Communication Skills (Year 3 Semester 1 Unit 1)

1. Reducing Anxiety & Stage Fear

Physical Symptoms: Shaking in hands, knees, and voice; "cottonmouth" (dryness of the mouth); sweating of palms.

Behavioral Differences: Anxious speakers place obstacles (lecterns, distance) between themselves and the audience. Confident speakers look at the audience directly, speak at normal rates, use lectern only to hold notes, and move body naturally.

• Hemsley & Doob (1978): Direct audience gaze increases credibility and persuasion.

• Lind & O'Barr (1979): Apprehensive speakers with more vocalized pauses (um, ah) are perceived as less persuasive.

• Miller, Maruyama, Beaber & Valone (1976): Exceptionally slow or powerless speakers are perceived as less knowledgeable and less credible.

2. Seven Elements of the Public Speaking Process

Public speaking acts as a transactional process consisting of: Source (Speaker), Message (verbal & non-verbal symbols), Receiver (Audience), Channel (delivery method), Feedback (audience response), Noise (interference), and Situation (specific environment context).

3. Six Keys to Confidence

  • Act Confident: Actions often change before attitudes do. Pretend to be in a role and proceed bravely.
  • Know your subject: Start with simple topics that don't need external research (such as an aspect of yourself).
  • Care about your subject: High levels of passion shift mental attention away from personal fear and into message delivery.
  • See classmates as friends: Understand they are peers facing the same pressure, wanting you to succeed.
  • See yourself as successful: Use positive "mental imaging" and visualize correct delivery.
  • Practice toward perfection: Duplicate classroom conditions, speak in empty rooms, get comfortable with the view.

4. Key Academic Terms & Vocabulary

HCA (High Communication Apprehension): Classified in 20% of college students. They choose isolated dorm spaces, sit in back corners of classroom, talk less, and show self-sabotaging communication avoidance.

Systematic Desensitization: Clinical therapy designed to slowly introduce small doses of the stimulus (e.g., imagining speaking -> standing at podium) to build immunity.

Self-Fulfilling Prophecy: Expecting failure leads to reduced practice, which causes poor execution, validating the initial fear.
